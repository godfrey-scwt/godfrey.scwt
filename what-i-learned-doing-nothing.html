What I learned doing nothing for a week

I took a week off with no plan. No trip, no project, no reading list. Just unstructured time, which turned out to be far more uncomfortable than I expected.

The first two days I filled with small errands, disguised as productivity, so I wouldn't have to sit with the discomfort of having nothing to do. By day three I'd run out of errands. That's when it actually started.

Boredom is a doorway, not a dead end

What I noticed, once the restlessness passed, was that my thoughts started to organize themselves without my help. Ideas I'd been circling for months suddenly had shape. Not because I worked on them, but because I finally gave them room.

Rest isn't the absence of thinking. It's thinking without an agenda.

I don't think I'll take unstructured weeks often — life doesn't usually allow it. But I'll remember this: the discomfort of doing nothing is front-loaded. It gets easier, and then it gets useful.
